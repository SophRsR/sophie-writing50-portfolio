REFLECTIVE INTRODUCTION

Reflective Introduction

Sophie Ren · Writing 50: Critical Reading and Rhetoric · Spring 2026

I came to Writing 50 with the same anxiety I've always had when writing an essay: I don't know where to start, and I don't know how to deliver my ideas effectively. I still don't have a clear answer to either question, and I've realized there's still a lot to practice, but my mindset about what good writing actually requires has shifted a lot throughout the course. I want to share two insights I've learned from this course — one from the perspective of how to structure your essay and the other about rhetorical choices. These are the most important changes I made that truly helped me "level up" as a writer.

Instead of asking yourself, "Is the idea amazing?", ask yourself, "Is it necessary?"

To be honest, I always found writing a hard task for most of my life. I don't lack ideas about writing, but the landing process is especially difficult: I would get dozens of ideas emerging simultaneously in my mind, having more ideas pop up as I go deeper into my paragraphs, and I couldn't resist adding all of them into my next paragraph. The result, as you can imagine, isn't beautiful at all. What I often get in the end is a bunch of messy paragraphs that are loosely connected, and I came to realize how far I've drifted away from my original purpose!

Taking Writing 50 somehow changed my approach to writing. I've learned so much about how to structure well before writing, avoid being distracted by new ideas, and only stick to those that are truly effective. In the end, all you want is a few important arguments that cut like a sharpened knife, instead of a million ideas that randomly land nowhere.

A good example would probably be my genre analysis. While I was thinking about the assignment before even writing it, I already jotted down a pile of evidence in my paragraph: Hannah Geist's red lips, Syd's crawling scene, color degradation, cannibalism… I have to admit, it was such an inspiring film and I really don't want to miss any point covered by the director. The pre-write assignment is actually what helped me out. I was "forced" to summarize my key arguments in each paragraph into a single sentence, and the painful summarization process revealed how messy my essay had become.

So I stared at the laptop and told myself, "OK, Sophie, you have to cut down the quantity of your ideas and focus on their quality." I created a checklist in my mind that I should keep fewer than 3 pieces of evidence in each of my paragraphs, and every piece I provide should not only be directly related to my central topic, but also provide a new aspect at the same time. The next thing I did was keep pressing the "delete" button. Is cannibalism related to bio-capitalism? It's probably one of the coolest elements in the film, but unfortunately, it's not. Are Hannah's death and Syd's illness providing a different perspective? Yes, they are, but a large part of their story feels repetitive. By continuing to question myself about these questions and criticizing my original work, I realized those "cool ideas" have become noise in my essay, which disguised my real theme about bio-capitalism. I ended up clearing a lot of ideas from my document, which is something that I was pretty reluctant to do at that time. Looking back at my whole writing process, cutting down those unnecessary points is what truly helped me develop my depth in writing, and I can't be more thankful for what I did!

Rhetorical choices should be based on who your target audience is

Another important lesson I learned from this course is: choose your rhetorical choices based on who your target audience is, not vice versa.

When I started off on my imitation project, I read through the document, spotted the word "multimodal," and the form "diary" just jumped out of my mind. Hey, a diary can be decorated with lots of stickers and photos, which perfectly satisfies the multimodal requirement. It allows me to write in a very personal tone, which means I can play with satire, metaphor, irony, and other kinds of rhetorical choices. What's more exciting is that it's super flexible — I can start with a blank page and be creative. Doesn't it sound like a perfect type of media?

I quickly finished a draft of my artifact. I came up with a horror story about how a girl became obsessed with cosmetic surgery and replaced every part of her organs, including her brain and heart. Everything feels great, until one day I received a comment from Dr. Speer saying:

Oh no… The comment just woke me up. I suddenly realized I've never considered who the audience is, and why they would read such a personal story.

I started doing some research on the web in terms of when people would post diaries of others, and how they can post them with a valid reason. I built a demographic chart in my mind in terms of the type of media — blog, magazine, legal documents, social media — and how they can influence audiences. Eventually, I decided to use X (Twitter), and what actually led to my decision is the other way around — thinking about who the audience is before deciding the type of media. Since my story centers around dehumanization in cosmetic surgery, my X post should focus on warning parents, surgeons, and other teenagers. I immediately had a clear storyline in my mind: Instead of letting the daughter post the diary herself, it could be a desperate mother who discovered her daughter's diary, followed her daughter's will to publish it, and wrote a post about this heartbreaking story. Everything started to click for me once I brought myself into the context and thought about how my target audience will view it in real life.

The approach also helped me with more detailed rhetorical choices, such as choosing my multimodal approach for the diary entries. I considered a lot in terms of how I want my audience to connect with the story in an emotional way, and used different approaches — such as irony, allusion, and juxtaposition — to bring audiences into an immersive experience. For example, inspired by the film Antiviral, I created a similar advertisement that looks like a "big brother" in George Orwell's 1984, but this time the "big brother" was the commercialized version of the girl herself. The use of allusion created an effective projection in terms of how the girl viewed herself through a more critical lens, even after her surgeries.

The process of applying all those rhetorical choices I learned from other people's work into my own work was a rewarding experience. Instead of being an audience, I started to move away from passively receiving information and tried to think from an analytical perspective. It really helped me form a better understanding of why those choices are actually effective. And more importantly, I began to think about how to make effective communication with my readers on top of what I want to write about.

So these are the two big takeaways I got from this course — a "less is more" strategy for a structured essay, and a deeper understanding of rhetorical choices. I enjoyed this course a lot, and I always feel that I've learned so much from rounds of feedback, revision, and peer review. Having honest and useful feedback from actual readers really helped me recognize my weaknesses and pushed me to think of better strategies for a higher-quality essay.
